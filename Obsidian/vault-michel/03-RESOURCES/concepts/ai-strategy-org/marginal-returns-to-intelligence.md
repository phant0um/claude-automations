# Marginal Returns to Intelligence

An economic framework proposed by [[03-RESOURCES/entities/dario-amodei]] for reasoning about AI-accelerated progress. Analogous to the economist's concept of "marginal returns to labor/land/capital" — asks: *how much does being smarter actually help with this task, on what timescale?*

## Limiting Factors (Complements to Intelligence)

When intelligence is very high, other factors become the binding constraints:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Speed of the outside world** | Cells, animals, hardware, and human systems move at fixed rates; many experiments require irreducible iteration time |
| **Data availability** | Raw data may be lacking; more intelligence doesn't conjure new particle accelerator readings |
| **Intrinsic complexity** | Chaotic systems (three-body problem, economic behavior) are irreducibly hard to predict |
| **Human constraints** | Laws, regulations, clinical trial bureaucracy, willingness to change — an aligned AI must respect these |
| **Physical laws** | Speed of light, thermodynamic limits, quantum tunneling — hard ceilings |

## Dynamic Picture

Constraints are not all permanent. Over time, intelligence itself routes around them:
- New experimental paradigms replace slow animal trials
- Better models reduce need for large human clinical trials
- AI-designed tools collect data that didn't exist before

Result: intelligence is initially heavily bottlenecked, then progressively less so — but some constraints (physical laws) are absolute.

## Application

Used to evaluate which domains AI can transform fastest (biology: high returns to intelligence) vs. slowest (economic policy: heavy human constraints, intrinsic complexity).
