# Analogical vs First-Principles Reasoning

The central tension in how people think about problems: copying existing patterns vs. deriving answers from fundamental truths.

## Analogical Reasoning

**What it is:** Looking at what exists and mapping it onto your situation. "This worked for them, so it'll work for me." "This is how it's always been done."

**Strengths:** Fast, low cognitive cost, reasonable for most daily decisions and learning contexts.

**Ceiling:** You can never get beyond what already exists by copying what already exists. Analogies become invisible — you forget you're copying and start thinking you're thinking.

**Risk:** Applying analogical reasoning to Type 1 decisions (irreversible, high-stakes) where the template was designed for someone else's situation, audience, and constraints.

## First-Principles Reasoning

**What it is:** Decomposing a problem to irreducible foundational truths and building up from there, without assuming existing conventions are correct. See [[03-RESOURCES/concepts/learning-cognition/first-principles-reasoning]].

**Strengths:** Breaks through the ceiling of what exists; enables genuinely novel solutions; reveals where conventional wisdom is just copies of copies.

**Cost:** Slow, mentally expensive, requires accepting discomfort (realizing how much you "know" you don't actually know).

**Best for:** Type 1 decisions — irreversible, high-stakes, where the outcome can't be undone.

## The Decision Heuristic

[[03-RESOURCES/entities/Jeff-Bezos]] framed this as Type 1 vs Type 2 decisions:

| Decision type | Reversibility | Recommended approach |
|---|---|---|
| Type 1 | Irreversible / high-stakes | First principles — question everything |
| Type 2 | Reversible / low-stakes | Analogy — go fast, copy what works |

The common mistake is **applying analogy to Type 1** decisions. Or wasting first-principles effort on Type 2.

## Munger's Frame

[[03-RESOURCES/entities/Charlie-Munger]]: "The trick is to have your brain work better than the other person's brain because it understands the most fundamental models." Most people's mental models aren't fundamental — they're copies of copies.
