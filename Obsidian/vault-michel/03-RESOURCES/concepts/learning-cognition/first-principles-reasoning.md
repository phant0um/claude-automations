# First Principles Reasoning

**First principles reasoning** is the practice of decomposing a problem down to its most fundamental, irreducible truths — and then rebuilding understanding or solutions from that foundation, without borrowing assumptions from existing conventions or analogies.

Coined formally by [[03-RESOURCES/entities/Aristotle]]: "the first basis from which a thing is known."

## Core Idea

Most thinking is **analogical**: look at what exists, copy it, assume it applies. This is fast and usually fine — but it has a ceiling. You can never transcend what exists by copying what exists.

First principles reasoning breaks that ceiling by asking: what is *actually* true here, independent of what everyone else does?

See also: [[03-RESOURCES/concepts/learning-cognition/analogical-vs-first-principles]]

## The Process

1. Surface all assumptions about the problem — write them down.
2. Ask "why?" three to four levels deep until hitting bedrock or an unexamined belief.
3. Separate known facts from inherited assumptions.
4. Imagine starting from zero — no templates, no best practices.
5. Derive the answer from fundamental truths upward.

This maps to the Socratic method: keep questioning until beliefs prove themselves or collapse.

## Canonical Examples

- **SpaceX:** [[03-RESOURCES/entities/Elon-Musk]] broke rocket pricing to material costs (2% of market price), revealing the 98% as reducible overhead — enabling a cost revolution rather than incremental improvement.
- **Ford:** Instead of "better horses," asked what problem transportation actually solves — leading to the automobile.
- **[[03-RESOURCES/entities/Richard-Feynman]]'s self-check:** "If I can't explain it simply, I don't really understand it."

## Limits

Slow and mentally expensive. Best reserved for **Type 1 decisions** (irreversible, high-stakes). For reversible everyday decisions, analogy is appropriate. Misapplying first principles to trivial decisions wastes cognitive resources; misapplying analogy to irreversible decisions is where real damage occurs.
