# Mental Models

**Mental models** are the cognitive frameworks people use to understand and reason about the world. Every decision passes through a mental model, whether consciously or not.

## The Core Problem

Most mental models aren't derived from first principles — they're inherited: copied from peers, absorbed from culture, or borrowed from someone else's context. As [[03-RESOURCES/entities/Charlie-Munger]] put it: "The human brain works in models. The trick is to have your brain work better than the other person's brain because it understands the most fundamental models."

Received wisdom that nobody examined is a mental model. Best practices built for someone else's situation are mental models. They work until they don't — and when they fail, you can't adapt because you never understood *why* they worked.

## Fundamental vs. Derived Models

| Type | Description | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Fundamental | Built from first principles, grounded in underlying truths | Can adapt to new situations |
| Derived / copied | Inherited from analogy, culture, or observation | Breaks when context shifts |

The goal is to hold more fundamental models, especially for high-stakes decisions.

## Relation to First Principles

[[03-RESOURCES/concepts/learning-cognition/first-principles-reasoning]] is the practice of building mental models from the ground up rather than inheriting them. [[03-RESOURCES/concepts/learning-cognition/analogical-vs-first-principles]] maps the tradeoffs.

[[03-RESOURCES/entities/Richard-Feynman]]'s check: if you can't explain it simply, you don't really have a model — you have a conclusion. Conclusions can't adapt; models can.
