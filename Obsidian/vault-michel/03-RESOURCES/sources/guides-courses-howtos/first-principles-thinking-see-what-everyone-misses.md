# First Principles Thinking: How to See What Everyone Else Misses

Thread essay by [@jaynitx](https://x.com/jaynitx) recounting a six-month product failure caused by pattern-matching instead of reasoning from fundamentals — and the framework that changed that.

## Definition

A **first principle** is a fundamental truth that cannot be broken down further — the bedrock that remains true regardless of what anyone else thinks or does. **First principles thinking** means starting from those bedrock truths and building up, rather than starting from what already exists and copying it.

The concept originates with [[03-RESOURCES/entities/Aristotle]], who defined a first principle as "the first basis from which a thing is known."

Its opposite is **reasoning by analogy**: looking at what exists, copying it, and assuming it applies to your situation.

## Framework / Steps

1. **Surface your assumptions.** Write out everything you "know" about the problem. Making assumptions visible is the first step to questioning them.
2. **Ask why repeatedly.** Like a toddler. Three or four levels of "why?" usually hits something fundamental or obviously unexamined.
3. **Separate known truths from assumptions.** Ask: "What do I actually know to be true vs. what am I assuming? Where did that belief come from?"
4. **Imagine starting from zero.** If you knew nothing about how it's "supposed" to work, what would you do?
5. **Rebuild from fundamentals.** Derive your answer from the bedrock truths, not from existing templates.

This is essentially the [[03-RESOURCES/concepts/socratic-method]] applied to problems: keep asking "why?" and "is that actually true?" until a belief proves itself or falls apart.

## Key Examples

### SpaceX Rocket Costs
[[03-RESOURCES/entities/Elon-Musk]] applied first principles to rocket pricing. Instead of accepting "rockets are expensive," he asked: what are rockets *made of*? The raw materials cost ~2% of market price. That 98% gap — manufacturing, labor, overhead, margin — was the design space for SpaceX's cost revolution.

### Henry Ford / Faster Horses
Analogy thinking: "People use horses — give them better horses." First principles: "What problem is being solved? Getting from A to B. What's the best solution?" The automobile. Copying keeps you inside the box of what already exists.

### Peter Thiel's Interview Question
"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — this is a proxy for where someone has done genuine first principles thinking and arrived at a non-consensus conclusion.

### Author's Personal Case
Spent six months building a product by copying competitors' models. When asked "what am I actually trying to achieve?" the answer diverged completely from what was being built. Applied first principles to content strategy: instead of "be everywhere" (analogy), derived: target platform = where audience actually is; constraint = time; conclusion = go deep on one or two platforms.

## When NOT to Use It

First principles thinking is slow and mentally expensive. [[03-RESOURCES/entities/Jeff-Bezos]]'s Type 1 / Type 2 decision framework is useful here:

- **Type 1 (irreversible, high-stakes):** use first principles — question every assumption.
- **Type 2 (reversible, low-stakes):** use analogy — go fast, copy what works.

The mistake is applying analogy to Type 1 decisions, or wasting first principles effort on Type 2.
